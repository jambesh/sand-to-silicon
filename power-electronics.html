⚡ Power Electronics

Chips that think are only half the story — something must feed them, and everything else, with clean energy. Power electronics converts and controls electrical power: from the watts sipped by your phone charger to the megawatts surging through an EV fast-charger. Explore the devices, the analog and digital ways to regulate power, and try the circuits yourself on a virtual breadboard.

PE · 01 CONCEPT

Processing Power, Not Information 🔌

Logic chips shuffle bits. Power chips shuffle joules — and the rules change completely.

The one big idea: the switch

A resistor that drops voltage burns the difference as heat. A perfect switch wastes nothing: fully ON it has no voltage across it, fully OFF it has no current through it — either way, P = V·I ≈ 0. All of modern power electronics is the art of switching fast and cleaning up the result with inductors and capacitors.

Ideal switch: ON → V≈0 · OFF → I≈0 · either way P = V·I ≈ 0

Analog vs digital power

Analog (linear) power regulates continuously — a transistor acts as a smart variable resistor (LDOs, amplifier output stages, precision references). Silent and clean, but it pays in heat. Digital (switching) power chops the input ON/OFF thousands to millions of times per second and filters the average — converters, inverters, motor drives. It's 90–98% efficient, and today the control loop itself is usually digital too: a microcontroller or DSP computing PWM duty cycles in real time.

  1. Convert — AC↔DC, DC↔DC, DC↔AC, AC↔AC: the four conversion families.
  2. Regulate — hold voltage/current steady as load and input swing.
  3. Protect — limit inrush, clamp spikes, shut down on faults.
  4. Control — analog feedback or digital loops (PWM, PID, and lately AI).

PE · 02 DEVICES

The Switch Toolbox — Diode to GaN 🧰

Every power circuit is built from a handful of semiconductor switches. Meet the family.

Four switches to rule them all

Diode — a one-way valve; conducts forward, blocks reverse. The simplest rectifier. Si MOSFET — a voltage-controlled switch: charge the gate and it conducts. Fast, easy to drive, king below ~650 V. IGBT — a hybrid built for brute force: handles thousands of volts and amps, but switches slowly. Trains, wind turbines, industrial drives. Wide-bandgap (SiC & GaN) — new materials whose atoms grip electrons ~3× harder than silicon, so devices can be thinner, faster and run hotter with less loss.

Why wide-bandgap changes everything

Higher switching frequency means smaller inductors and capacitors — that's why a 65 W GaN charger is a third the size of the old silicon brick. SiC survives 1,200+ V with tiny losses — that's why EVs moving to 800 V batteries put SiC in the traction inverter and gain ~5% driving range for free.

PE · 03 ANALOG POWER

Analog: The Linear Regulator 🎚️

Smooth, silent, simple — and it pays for all three in heat.

A transistor as a smart resistor

A linear regulator (like the classic 7805 or a modern LDO) puts a transistor in series with the load and continuously adjusts how hard it conducts, so the output stays at exactly Vout no matter what the input does. An error amplifier compares the output against a reference and closes the loop thousands of times per second — pure analog feedback.

ηlinear = Vout / Vin  ·  Pheat = (Vin − Vout) × Iload

Drop 12 V to 3.3 V at 1 A and the regulator dissipates 8.7 W — more heat than useful power! That's why linear is reserved for low currents and small voltage drops: audio circuits, sensor rails, RF stages, and the final clean-up stage after a noisy switcher.

  1. Reference — a bandgap circuit makes a rock-stable ~1.2 V.
  2. Compare — the error amp measures Vout against it.
  3. Adjust — the pass transistor conducts more or less, instantly.
  4. Repeat — continuous, no switching, no ripple, no EMI.

PE · 04 DIGITAL POWER

Digital: The Buck Converter Playground 🕹️

The most-manufactured power circuit on Earth — billions made every year. Drive one yourself.

Chop, then smooth

The buck converter steps voltage down with almost no loss. A MOSFET chops the input at 100 kHz–2 MHz; the fraction of time it's ON is the duty cycle D. The inductor resists current changes and the capacitor holds the voltage steady, so the load sees only the average:

Vout = D × Vin   (e.g. 50% duty of 12 V → 6 V)
Vout ≈ 6.0 V ripple ±0.2 V η ≈ 95%

The digital control loop

In modern designs a tiny controller measures Vout every switching cycle and nudges D via a PID algorithm — power regulation as a software problem. That's "digital power": the same circuit, but the brain is code. It also opens the door to something smarter than PID — which is exactly where the AI module picks up.

Virtual Breadboard Lab 🧑‍🔬

The circuits above, wired the way you'd actually build them — poke them and watch the instruments respond.

Vout— duty—

A microcontroller drives a MOSFET gate with PWM; the MOSFET switches the LED. Average current — and brightness — follows duty cycle. This is exactly how screen backlights and LED bulbs dim.

Four diodes flip the negative half of the AC wave upward; the capacitor fills the gaps. Bigger capacitor → smaller ripple. This front-end lives inside every wall adapter ever made.
