STAGE 01 · RAW MATERIAL

From Sand to Silicon 🏖️

Silicon is the second most abundant element in Earth's crust — but nature never hands it over pure.

Not just any sand

Chips begin with high-purity quartz (SiO₂) — typically mined quartzite rock rather than beach sand, which carries too many impurities. The quartz is crushed, washed and graded before smelting.

The carbothermic reduction

Quartz is mixed with carbon sources (coke, coal, wood chips) and fed into a submerged electric-arc furnace running at about 2,000 °C. The carbon strips oxygen away from the SiO₂:

SiO₂ + 2C → Si + 2CO ↑

Molten silicon is tapped from the bottom of the furnace and solidified. The result is metallurgical-grade silicon (MG-Si) — about 98–99% pure. Great for aluminium alloys and silicones… hopeless for chips, which need a billion times better.

  1. Mine & crush high-purity quartzite (>99% SiO₂).
  2. Blend with carbon reductants in controlled ratios.
  3. Smelt at ~2,000 °C in an electric arc furnace.
  4. Tap & cast molten silicon into MG-Si chunks (98%+).
QuartziteSiO₂Arc furnaceMG-SiCarbothermic reduction
💡 Did you know?
Producing 1 tonne of silicon consumes roughly 11–13 MWh of electricity — which is why smelters cluster near cheap hydropower.
STAGE 02 · PURIFICATION

Ultra-Purification — The Siemens Process ⚗️

From 98% to 99.9999999% (“nine nines”): fewer than one foreign atom per billion silicon atoms.

Why so extreme?

A single misplaced impurity atom can change how silicon conducts electricity. Since doping (Stage 08) works at parts-per-million levels, the starting material must be far cleaner than anything doping will add — hence electronic-grade silicon (EG-Si) at 9N–11N purity.

Distill it as a gas

You can't filter a solid, but you can distill a liquid. MG-Si is reacted with hydrogen chloride to form trichlorosilane (TCS), a liquid that boils at just 31.8 °C:

Si + 3HCl → SiHCl₃ + H₂   (≈300 °C)

TCS is fractionally distilled — repeatedly — until impurities are gone. Then, in a Siemens reactor, purified TCS decomposes on glowing 1,100 °C silicon rods, depositing ultra-pure polysilicon:

SiHCl₃ + H₂ → Si ↓ + 3HCl
  1. Chlorinate: MG-Si + HCl → trichlorosilane gas.
  2. Distill repeatedly — impurities have different boiling points.
  3. Deposit pure Si on heated rods (CVD) in the Siemens reactor.
  4. Harvest polysilicon rods, broken into chunks for crystal growth.
TrichlorosilaneFractional distillationSiemens reactorPolysilicon9N purity
💡 Scale of purity
9N purity is like finding one specific person among the entire population of Earth — eight times over. If a football stadium were silicon atoms, an impurity would be one grain of dust.
STAGE 03 · CRYSTAL GROWTH

Growing the Crystal — Czochralski Pulling 💎

Pure isn't enough. Every atom must also sit in one perfect, repeating lattice — a single crystal.

Why single-crystal?

Polysilicon is a jumble of tiny crystals; the boundaries between them scatter electrons and break transistors. Chips need monocrystalline silicon — one unbroken atomic lattice from edge to edge.

The Czochralski (CZ) method

Polysilicon chunks are melted at 1,414 °C in a quartz crucible. A thumbnail-sized seed crystal is dipped into the melt, then withdrawn at millimetres per minute while both seed and crucible counter-rotate. Atoms freeze onto the seed, copying its perfect lattice. Over ~30 hours this grows a gleaming ingot (boule) up to 300 mm across and 2 m long.

  1. Melt polysilicon (+ precise dopant dose) in a quartz crucible under argon.
  2. Dip a perfect seed crystal into the melt surface.
  3. Neck down to a thin filament to squeeze out dislocations (Dash necking).
  4. Pull & rotate slowly — diameter is controlled by pull speed and temperature.
  5. Taper off and cool: a single-crystal ingot weighing ~200–300 kg.
CzochralskiSeed crystalBoule / IngotMonocrystallineFloat-zone (FZ)
💡 Did you know?
The method was discovered by accident in 1916 when Polish chemist Jan Czochralski dipped his pen into molten tin instead of his inkwell — and pulled out a single-crystal filament.
STAGE 04 · WAFERING

Wafer Shaping — Slice, Grind, Polish 🪚

Turning a 300 kg crystal cylinder into mirror-flat discs less than a millimetre thick.

Slicing

The ingot is ground to an exact diameter, given a notch to mark crystal orientation, then sliced by a multi-wire diamond saw — a single 100+ km wire wound into a web of hundreds of parallel cutting lines, slicing the whole ingot at once into ~775 µm wafers.

Making it perfect

Sawn wafers are rough and stressed. A sequence of lapping → edge rounding → chemical etching → CMP polishing removes damage until the surface is flat to within nanometres. Many wafers then receive a defect-free epitaxial layer — a freshly grown crystal film to build devices in.

  1. Grind & notch the ingot to a precise 300 mm diameter.
  2. Wire-saw into slices (~⅓ of the crystal becomes sawdust, called kerf).
  3. Lap & round edges to remove saw marks and prevent chipping.
  4. Etch away subsurface damage chemically.
  5. CMP polish to an atomically smooth mirror; clean and inspect.
Wire sawKerf lossLappingCMPEpitaxyNotch / flat
💡 How flat is flat?
If a 300 mm wafer were scaled to the size of a football field, its tallest bump would be about the thickness of a coin. Total thickness variation is held under ~1 µm.
STAGE 05 · DESIGN

Chip Design & Photomasks 📐

Before the fab touches a wafer, the chip is born as code — and shipped to the fab as a set of masks.

From idea to layout

Engineers describe chip behaviour in hardware description languages (Verilog/VHDL). EDA tools then synthesize that code into logic gates, place millions of standard cells, and route the wires between them — all while meeting timing, power and area targets. The final layout ships as GDSII/OASIS geometry.

Photomasks: the stencils

Each chip layer becomes a photomask — a quartz plate with chrome patterns written by electron beams, usually at 4× the final size. A modern chip needs 60–100+ masks. Patterns are pre-distorted (OPC) so that after the physics of light does its worst, the printed shape is what designers intended.

  1. Architect & RTL — describe function in Verilog/VHDL.
  2. Verify — simulate trillions of cycles; bugs in silicon cost millions.
  3. Synthesize → place → route into physical geometry.
  4. Sign-off — timing, power, DRC/LVS physical checks.
  5. Tape-out — write each layer onto quartz-chrome masks by e-beam.
RTL / VerilogEDAPlace & routeGDSIITape-outOPCReticle
💡 Did you know?
A full EUV mask set for a leading-edge chip can cost tens of millions of dollars — before a single wafer is printed. “Tape-out” is named after the magnetic tapes once used to send designs to the fab.
STAGE 06 · PATTERNING

Photolithography — Printing with Light 🔦

The single most important (and expensive) step in the fab, repeated ~100 times per wafer.

A photographic process at atomic scale

The wafer is spin-coated with light-sensitive photoresist, then a scanner projects the mask image onto it — shrunk 4× through the world's most precise optics. Exposed resist changes chemistry; a developer washes it away, leaving a stencil on the wafer for etching or implantation.

The wavelength war

Feature size is limited by the wavelength of light. The industry marched from mercury lamps (436→365 nm) to deep-UV excimer lasers (248→193 nm, plus immersion in water), and finally to EUV at 13.5 nm — light so extreme it's made by vaporising tin droplets with a laser 50,000 times a second, and only mirrors can focus it.

  1. Coat — spin photoresist into a uniform, ultra-thin film; soft-bake.
  2. Expose — scanner steps across the wafer, flashing the mask image die by die.
  3. Develop — dissolve exposed (or unexposed) resist; inspect overlay.
  4. Etch / implant — transfer the resist pattern into the layer below.
  5. Strip — remove remaining resist; repeat for the next layer, ~100×.
PhotoresistScanner / StepperDUV 193iEUV 13.5 nmOverlayMulti-patterning
💡 Did you know?
An EUV scanner costs ~$200–400 million, ships in 40 freight containers, and its mirrors are so smooth that scaled to the size of Germany the biggest bump would be under a millimetre.
STAGE 07 · ETCH & DEPOSITION

Sculpting with Plasma, Painting with Atoms 🌫️

Chips are built layer by layer: add a film, pattern it, carve it, repeat — hundreds of times.

Etch: removing material

Wet etch uses chemicals (fast, cheap, but rounds corners). Dry / plasma etch uses reactive ions accelerated vertically at the wafer — it carves razor-straight vertical walls, enabling extreme structures like the 50:1 aspect-ratio channel holes in 3D NAND.

Deposition: adding material

Films go down by thermal oxidation (growing SiO₂ in a furnace), CVD (gases react on the surface), PVD/sputtering (atoms knocked off a metal target), and ALD — which deposits one atomic layer at a time for the thinnest, most conformal films like gate dielectrics.

  1. Grow/deposit a film: oxide, nitride, poly-Si, or metal.
  2. Pattern it with lithography (Stage 06).
  3. Etch — plasma ions transfer the pattern into the film.
  4. Clean & planarize (CMP) so the next layer starts flat.
RIE plasma etchThermal oxidationCVDPVD / sputterALDCMPAnisotropy
💡 Cleanroom scale
A fab cleanroom (ISO Class 1) has < 10 particles per m³ — thousands of times cleaner than a hospital operating theatre. People are the dirtiest thing in it, hence the bunny suits.
STAGE 08 · DOPING

Doping & Ion Implantation 🎯

Pure silicon barely conducts. Sprinkling in the right atoms — about one per million — brings it to life.

N-type and P-type

Silicon has 4 outer electrons. Implant phosphorus or arsenic (5 electrons) and you get a spare electron — N-type. Implant boron (3 electrons) and you get a missing one, a “hole” — P-type. Put N and P side by side and you get a junction that conducts one way only: the building block of every transistor.

The atomic cannon

An ion implanter ionizes dopant gas, accelerates the ions through up to hundreds of kilovolts, filters them by mass with a magnet, and scans the beam across the wafer. Photoresist blocks the ions everywhere except where transistors need them.

  1. Mask the wafer — resist shields all but the target regions.
  2. Ionize & accelerate dopants; a magnet selects the exact species.
  3. Implant — energy controls depth, dose controls strength.
  4. Anneal (~1,000 °C for seconds) to heal crystal damage and activate dopants.
N-type / P-typeIon implanterDose & energyRTA annealp-n junctionMOSFET
💡 Why it matters
A modern CPU transistor switches on and off ~5 billion times per second, controlled entirely by these implanted regions — each containing just a few hundred dopant atoms.
STAGE 09 · INTERCONNECT (BEOL)

Interconnects — Wiring the City 🕸️

Transistors are useless until connected. Above them rises a skyscraper of copper wiring.

Front-end vs back-end

Everything so far — transistors in silicon — is the FEOL (front end of line). The BEOL (back end of line) builds 10–18 layers of metal wiring above them: short, dense wires at the bottom for local connections; wide fast highways at the top for power and clocks.

Damascene copper

Copper can't be plasma-etched easily, so the industry flipped the process: etch trenches into insulator, electroplate copper into them, then polish the excess flat with CMP. This dual-damascene flow, plus barrier layers of tantalum, repeats for every metal level. Low-k dielectrics between wires cut parasitic capacitance so signals stay fast.

  1. Deposit insulating dielectric (low-k) over the transistors.
  2. Etch via holes + wire trenches (dual damascene).
  3. Line with a tantalum barrier + copper seed.
  4. Electroplate copper to overfill; CMP polish flat.
  5. Repeat 10–18× — each level checked by metrology.
FEOL / BEOLDual damasceneCopper platingLow-k dielectricViasCMP
💡 Did you know?
Stretched end to end, the copper wiring inside a single modern GPU die would run over 100 km — packed into a fingernail of silicon.
STAGE 10 · WAFER TEST

Wafer Test & Probing 🔬

Before spending money on packaging, every die is interrogated — while still on the wafer.

Two checkpoints

WAT / PCM (wafer acceptance test) first measures special test structures in the scribe lines — transistor thresholds, resistances, leakage — to confirm the process itself is healthy. Then wafer sort (CP) functionally tests every individual die.

How probing works

A prober aligns the wafer under a probe card — a bed of microscopic tungsten needles matching the die's bond pads. The ATE (automatic test equipment) drives test patterns through the needles at full speed, checking logic, memory, leakage and power. Bad dies are logged in a wafer map; only good dies proceed to packaging.

  1. WAT/PCM — parametric tests on scribe-line structures.
  2. Align wafer optically; touch down with ~1–3 g force per needle.
  3. Run patterns — scan chains, BIST, functional & parametric tests.
  4. Bin each die: pass, fail type, or “needs laser repair” (memories).
  5. Ink / map — the electronic wafer map guides die pick after sawing.
Probe cardWafer sort / CPWAT / PCMATEScan / BISTBin mapKnown-good die
💡 Did you know?
Advanced probe cards carry 50,000+ needles and cost more than a luxury car. Testing can consume up to 25–40% of total chip cost — test time is money.
STAGE 11 · YIELD

Yield Engineering — The Economics of Chips 📊

Yield = the fraction of dies that work. It decides whether a chip makes or loses money.

Defects are inevitable

Every wafer picks up random defects — a dust particle, a void in copper, a litho hiccup. If a defect lands on a die, that die usually fails. So yield falls exponentially with die area: big dies are gambling with worse odds, which is why huge GPU dies are so expensive and why chiplets are on the rise.

The math

With defect density D₀ (defects/cm²) and die area A, the classic models are:

Poisson: Y = e−A·D₀   ·   Murphy: Y = ((1 − e−A·D₀) / (A·D₀))²

Mature processes reach D₀ ≈ 0.05–0.1/cm²; a brand-new node might start at 0.5+ and improve every quarter — that improvement curve is the yield ramp, and it's where fabs win or lose billions.

Defect density D₀Poisson / Murphy modelYield rampParametric yieldRedundancy & repairChiplets
💡 Rule of thumb
Double the die area and yield roughly squares (e.g. 80% → 64%). That's why a die 2× bigger can cost far more than 2× as much.

STAGE 12 · ASSEMBLY

Dicing & Packaging 📦

A bare die is fragile and useless. The package gives it power, signals, protection and cooling.

Singulation

The wafer is first ground thin (from ~775 µm down to 100–300 µm), mounted on sticky tape, then diced along the scribe lines by a diamond blade or laser. A pick-and-place robot lifts only the dies the wafer map marked good.

Connecting die to world

Old school: wire bonding — gold/copper wires looped from die pads to package leads, stitched at 20+ wires per second. Modern high-performance: flip-chip — the die is flipped onto solder micro-bumps. Cutting edge: 2.5D/3D advanced packaging stacks dies on silicon interposers with through-silicon vias (TSVs) — how HBM memory sits next to AI GPUs.

  1. Backgrind the wafer thin; mount on dicing tape.
  2. Dice with diamond saw or stealth laser.
  3. Attach good dies to substrate (epoxy or solder).
  4. Connect — wire bond or flip-chip reflow; underfill for strength.
  5. Encapsulate in mould compound; attach lid/heat spreader; ball-mount.
BackgrindingDicingWire bondFlip-chipBGATSV / 2.5D / 3DUnderfill
💡 Did you know?
Packaging has become the new battleground: technologies like TSMC's CoWoS — gluing GPU + HBM stacks onto a silicon interposer — are so in demand for AI that they, not wafers, have been the supply bottleneck.
STAGE 13 · FINAL TEST

Final Test, Burn-In & Binning ✅

The last gate before your chip ships — and where “i7 vs i5” is often literally decided.

Testing the packaged chip

Packaging itself can introduce faults, so every chip is tested again in a socket: functional patterns, I/O speed, power draw — across temperature corners from freezing to scorching. Burn-in stresses chips at elevated voltage and heat to trigger early-life failures before customers ever see them (the “bathtub curve”).

Binning: nature meets marketing

No two dies are identical — atomic-level variation means some run faster or leak less. Speed binning sorts them: the best become flagship parts, others are down-clocked into cheaper SKUs, and dies with a defective core may be sold with that core disabled (that's often what a 6-core version of an 8-core chip is).

  1. Final test — full functional + parametric screen in a socket.
  2. Burn-in — accelerated aging to catch infant mortality.
  3. Speed/power binning — grade each chip into an SKU.
  4. Mark, tape & reel — laser-mark, package for shipment. 🎉

Fab Glossary 📖

The vocabulary you'll hear in any fab, distilled.

Wafer
A thin polished disc of single-crystal silicon (usually 200/300 mm) on which chips are fabricated.
Die (plural: dies/dice)
One individual chip rectangle on a wafer, before packaging.
Fab / Foundry
A semiconductor factory. A foundry (TSMC, Samsung, GlobalFoundries) manufactures designs for other companies.
Node
A process generation ("3 nm", "N5"). Once a literal gate length, now mostly a marketing label for a density/performance tier.
Photoresist
A light-sensitive polymer coating that captures the mask image during lithography.
Reticle / Mask
A quartz plate carrying one layer's pattern at 4× scale, projected onto the wafer.
Doping
Adding trace impurity atoms (B, P, As) to silicon to control its conductivity type and strength.
FEOL / BEOL
Front End Of Line (transistors in silicon) vs Back End Of Line (metal wiring above them).
CMP
Chemical-Mechanical Planarization — polishing each layer flat so the next one can be printed in focus.
Yield
The percentage of dies (or chips) that pass testing. The single most-watched number in any fab.
Wafer sort / CP
Electrical test of every die on the wafer using a probe card, before dicing.
ATE
Automatic Test Equipment — machines that drive test patterns into chips and grade the results.
Binning
Sorting tested chips by speed/power into different product SKUs.
Scribe line
Narrow streets between dies, used for test structures and consumed when the wafer is sawn.
Chiplet
A small die designed to be combined with others in one package — a yield and cost strategy.
Cleanroom class
Air-purity rating; ISO 1 allows <10 particles/m³ — thousands of times cleaner than an operating room.
